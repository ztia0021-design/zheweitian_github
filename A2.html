How can the climate affect Australia?

Exploring the impact of climate on Australia's diverse communities

Climate Type Distribution Map of Australia

Australia's state climates vary significantly: Queensland has tropical rainforest in the north and subtropical humidity in the south; New South Wales is mostly temperate maritime with dry inland areas; Victoria is temperate with four distinct seasons; Tasmania has a cool, humid temperate maritime climate; South Australia is Mediterranean with hot, dry summers; Western Australia has a Mediterranean south and tropical savanna north; the Northern Territory has tropical monsoonal weather in the north and desert climate in the center; the Australian Capital Territory is temperate with cold winters and hot summers.

Distribution of cities in climate zones

Australia's capital cities span five distinct climate zones. Darwin has a tropical monsoon climate with high rainfall. Brisbane and Sydney have subtropical climates. Perth and Adelaide have Mediterranean climates with dry summers. Melbourne, Canberra, and Hobart have cool temperate climates with relatively even rainfall distribution. Elevation Distribution in Australia

Australia is generally low and flat, with an average elevation of about 330 meters, making it the lowest continent except for Europe. In the east lie the Great Dividing Range, with the highest peak, Mount Kosciuszko, reaching 2,228 meters. The central region consists of vast plains, including the Great Artesian Basin, with most areas below 200 meters in elevation. The west is a plateau region, generally flat, with elevations ranging between 200 and 500 meters. The overall topography is characterized by "high in the east, low in the center, and gently elevated in the west," lacking any major mountain ranges.

Population distribution and city distribution in Australia

Australia has a very low population density, with only about 3 people per square kilometer, and the distribution is highly uneven. The majority of the population is concentrated along the eastern coast and in the southeastern region, particularly in major cities such as Sydney, Melbourne, and Brisbane. Vast inland and western areas are sparsely populated, with large deserts almost uninhabited. The tropical north and some parts of the southern coast also have relatively small populations. Overall, the pattern is "dense in the southeast, sparse elsewhere," making Australia one of the most unevenly populated countries in the world.

Population distribution of each city

The population of Australian capital cities varies significantly. Sydney is the largest with over 5.3 million residents, followed by Melbourne with over 5 million. Brisbane, Perth, and Adelaide have populations between 1-2.5 million, while Canberra, Hobart, and Darwin have smaller populations.
